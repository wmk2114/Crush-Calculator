step 1 of 3

what's the chance your crush likes you?

First, let's come up with a probability for how likely it is your crush is into you.

Odds are you're not sure about what the exact number is, but you have an educated guess. Below, you can create a graph that reflects your uncertainty, using what's called the beta distribution.

The beta distribution takes two numbers, α and β. The mean of your distribution is α/(α+β). If you're super confident about how likely it is that your crush likes you, use higher values of both α and β. If you're less certain, use smaller ones.

A good starting point: If you think the odds are around 50/50, try α = 5, β = 5. More optimistic? Try α = 7, β = 3. Preparing for the worst? Try α = 2, β = 8.

Higher α relative to β → more optimistic

Higher β relative to α → more pessimistic

what would it be worth?

Imagine you knew your crush liked you back. How much would someone need to pay you not to ask them out? That value — the amount you'd be giving up — is how we're going to measure the potential benefits of asking someone out.

Again, you might not be certain about how much money it'd actually take to stop you from asking out your crush. To reflect that uncertainty, we're going to use what's called a normal distribution. The mean, μ, is your best guess of how much money it'd take. If you're confident about how much money you'd need not to ask out your crush, use a smaller standard deviation, σ, relative to your mean. If you're less confident, use a greater value for σ.

why this recommendation?

the calculator runs a bunch of simulations to estimate the expected value of asking your crush out. each simulation picks a likely chance your crush likes you, a likely benefit if they do, and a likely cost if they don't.

the expected value formula is: e = b · p − c · (1 − p). that means the value of asking out is the benefit times the chance they like you, minus the cost times the chance they don't.

running lots of simulations helps show whether the choice is usually positive or usually negative, instead of trusting just one guess.

remember that this calculator only knows what you tell it.
consider what numbers you used and whether they're reasonable.
